Between the Field and the Network

A Midterm Critical Reflection on the Foundations of Embodied Cognition
Ian McDermid  |  Perception & Action

I entered this course already sympathetic to connectionism and embodied cognition. I came in with William James and Karl Popper as familiar touchstones, with Heidegger's (1927/1962) insistence that we are always already embedded in a world of practical concern somewhere in the background, and with a loose conviction that neural networks and distributed systems offer the best computational vocabulary for simulating human behavior. What I had not done was trace that conviction backward through its historical foundations or forward through its empirical consequences. The readings and discussions in this course forced both movements at once. Encountering Marcus (2001, 2018) as a sustained critic of connectionism, and then reading him alongside Goldinger et al. (2016) and Mahon (2015), produced something I did not expect: not a reversal of my position but an uncomfortable sharpening of it. The result is a perspective that I cannot yet name but can begin to describe, a hybrid theoretical commitment that values the ontology of field theories and the epistemology of falsificationism, and that finds itself caught between them.

What: The Landscape of the Debate

The embodied cognition (EC) debate, as I have encountered it this semester, is not a single argument but a constellation of overlapping disputes. Goldinger et al. (2016) offered the broadest indictment, arguing that EC is theoretically vacuous for the vast majority of cognitive phenomena, that its core principles are either trivially true or unfalsifiably vague, and that it cannot generate the kind of formal models that cognitive science requires. Mahon (2015) sharpened the critique by introducing a specific alternative hypothesis: that activation spreads from amodal conceptual representations to sensorimotor systems, such that every finding marshaled in favor of EC is equally consistent with classical accounts. Wołoszyn and Hohol (2017) countered by reframing EC as a Lakatosian research program whose hard core should be evaluated by the problemshifts it generates, not by whether it explains the Stroop effect. Zwaan (2021) redirected attention to methodological rigor, noting that the action-sentence compatibility effect (Glenberg & Kaschak, 2002) failed to replicate across 18 laboratories. Chemero (2003) did something different from all of them: he extended Gibson's (1979) theory of affordances by arguing that affordances are relations between animal abilities and situational features, grounded in the radical empiricism of William James. Across these readings, two issues have come to organize my thinking. The first is ontological: what are affordances, and what kind of science follows from treating them as real? The second is about representational format: does cognition require internal representations, or can it be fully explained by the dynamics of organism-environment coupling?

So What: Historical Roots and Unresolved Tensions

What struck me about Chemero's (2003) argument was not the affordance theory itself but the lineage he traced. Through Heft (2001), Chemero linked Gibson's ecological psychology to James's (1912/1976) radical empiricism, a philosophical position in which relations are as real and as perceivable as the things they relate. This is not merely an abstract metaphysical commitment. It is a claim about the ontological furniture of the world, and it places ecological psychology squarely within the tradition of Jamesian natural science: the view that psychology should study the organism-environment system as a unified phenomenon, not decompose it into internal representations and external stimuli. The resonance with Heidegger (1927/1962) is difficult to miss; Heidegger's concept of being-in-the-world similarly refuses to separate subject from environment, and Gibson himself acknowledged the phenomenological tradition even as he departed from it methodologically. I recognized in this lineage something I had encountered independently in social psychology. Kurt Lewin's (1936, 1951) Gestalt field theory formalized a similar intuition decades before Gibson, positing that behavior is a function of the total psychological field, B = f(P, E), where the person and the environment are not separable causes but interdependent regions of a single topological space. Lewin was not an ecological psychologist, and his mathematical formalism was more ambitious than Gibson's, but the structural parallel is difficult to ignore: both rejected elementism, both insisted on the irreducibility of the organism-environment relation, and both drew on Gestalt principles to argue that the whole is not merely the sum of its parts.

Gibson's (1979) affordance theory, as refined by Chemero (2003), became the ontological foundation for much of what is now called embodied cognition. The transition from field theory to EC, however, introduced commitments that the original field theorists did not share, particularly the claim that cognition does not require internal representations. It is at this juncture that Marcus (2001) becomes indispensable as a dissenting voice. Marcus argued that connectionist networks fail to capture the systematicity of human cognition, the fact that understanding is compositional and rule-sensitive in ways that distributed activation patterns alone cannot explain. Marcus (2018) extended these criticisms to deep learning, contending that even contemporary neural networks remain brittle and incapable of genuine abstraction. The charge, in both cases, is that a theoretically attractive framework overgeneralizes from a narrow set of successes.

The tension between these positions maps onto a deeper epistemological divide, one that extends to how researchers analyze data. Goldinger et al. (2016) applied what amounts to a Popperian standard: if EC cannot generate falsifiable predictions across the core phenomena of cognitive science, it fails as a scientific theory. The classical tradition that Goldinger et al. defend favors reaction time distributions, drift-diffusion models, and factorial designs that isolate variables. Dynamical systems researchers, by contrast, look for signatures of complexity in continuous behavioral time series: 1/f scaling, recurrence structure, attractor dynamics. These are not just different statistical tools; they reflect different ontological commitments about whether cognition is best described as a sequence of discrete operations or as the continuous evolution of a high-dimensional system. Wołoszyn and Hohol (2017) countered with Lakatos (1976), arguing that research programs are not falsified in the way individual hypotheses are; they degenerate or progress on the basis of the auxiliary hypotheses they spawn. Both positions have merit. Karl Popper (1959) himself acknowledged the danger: auxiliary hypotheses can immunize a theory against refutation indefinitely, which is precisely the trap that EC risks when it appeals to affordances or situatedness to explain away negative results. Yet Popper's own evolutionary epistemology, his claim that scientific theories evolve by conjecture and refutation much as organisms evolve by variation and selection, underestimates the degree to which theoretical traditions persist not because they survive falsification but because they structure the questions researchers know how to ask. The question, then, is not whether EC is true or false in some absolute sense but whether the problemshifts it generates are progressive. Marcus's (2001, 2018) critiques suggest they are not, at least not without substantial architectural concessions that would blur the boundary between connectionist and classical accounts. Van Orden et al. (2001) gestured toward exactly this kind of hybrid in their challenge to double-dissociation logic, arguing that the modularity assumption underlying classical cognitive neuropsychology is itself unfounded, but doing so from within a framework that still required computational precision.

Now What: Implications for My Own Research

This is where the reflection becomes personal, and where I have the most to learn. My thesis research asks whether we can collect human behavioral training data for agent-based model (ABM) calibration while simultaneously conducting cognitive performance research. The specific task examines the effect of AI-related threat to employment on premature exploitation and re-exploration (cognitive inflexibility), and whether intolerance for ambiguity potentiates that effect. Every construct in that question presupposes representational commitments that the EC debate forces me to examine. "Cognitive inflexibility" implies a measurable deviation from an optimal decision policy. "Intolerance for ambiguity" implies stable individual differences in information processing. "Agent-based model calibration" implies that human behavior can be parameterized and simulated. None of these constructs sit comfortably within a radical embodied framework that rejects internal representations.

Yet I am not a classical computationalist either. I do not believe that minds work like rule-based systems with memory stored in discrete bins. I am drawn to the neural network ABM as a modeling tool precisely because it preserves the distributed, nonlinear, context-sensitive character of cognition that field theorists from Lewin through Gibson to Thelen and Smith (1994) have insisted upon. Thelen et al. (2001) demonstrated the power of this commitment concretely: their dynamic field theory of infant perseverative reaching showed that the A-not-B error, long treated as evidence for or against innate object concepts, could be explained by the coupled dynamics of perception, memory, and action unfolding in real time, with no need to posit static mental structures. That result matters for my own work because it demonstrates that a formally specified, neurally grounded dynamical model can generate falsifiable predictions about goal-directed behavior without reverting to classical symbolic architecture. The challenge Marcus (2001, 2018) poses for me is not that connectionism is wrong but that it is incomplete: it needs the kind of structural sensitivity that classical architectures provide without collapsing into the rigid modularity that Van Orden et al. (2001) rightly questioned. Hofstadter (1979) captured something of this tension when he argued that meaning in formal systems arises from self-referential loops that cannot be reduced to either their symbolic rules or their substrate, a point that neither pure connectionism nor pure computationalism has fully absorbed. What I cannot yet articulate is what that hybrid looks like in practice, as a computational architecture that could underwrite the agent-based models I want to build.

This course has clarified for me that the question is not new. James wanted psychology to be a natural science without abandoning the richness of experience. Lewin wanted mathematical formalism without elementist reduction. Gibson wanted realism about meaning without retreating into mentalism. Each of these thinkers failed, in one sense, because the tools available to them could not fully support their ambitions. But the ambition itself, a science of cognition that is formally rigorous and ecologically valid, computationally tractable and ontologically honest, is the right ambition. Marcus's dissent is what keeps that ambition honest. Without it, the appeal of connectionism and EC risks becoming what Goldinger et al. (2016) accused it of being: an enthusiasm that mistakes theoretical elegance for explanatory power. With it, the demand for falsifiable, formally specified hybrid architectures becomes not a retreat to classical cognitivism but the next progressive problemshift in Lakatos's sense. Zwaan (2021) is right that none of this progress will hold unless the field commits to preregistration, open materials, shared code, and data repositories that make replication straightforward rather than heroic. If I want my own work to contribute to that problemshift, the research I produce must be transparent enough for someone else to reproduce it, critique it, and build on it. That is the direction I want my doctoral work to take, and this course has given me the historical vocabulary and the methodological conscience to say so.
